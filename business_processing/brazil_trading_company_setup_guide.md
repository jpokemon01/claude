## 1. Legal Business Structures

### EIRELI — No Longer Available

EIRELI (Empresa Individual de Responsabilidade Limitada) was abolished by Law No. 14,195 of August 26, 2021. Existing EIRELIs were automatically converted to SLU (Sociedade Limitada Unipessoal). Do not attempt to register as EIRELI — the option no longer exists in the REDESIM system.

### SLU — Sociedade Limitada Unipessoal (Single-Member Ltda)

The modern successor to EIRELI. A single-shareholder limited liability company. No minimum capital required. The sole owner can be a foreigner. This is effectively just an Ltda with one member.

### Ltda — Sociedade Limitada (Limited Liability Company)

The most common business structure in Brazil and the **recommended choice** for a small trading company.

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Minimum shareholders | 1 (since 2021 reforms — SLU variant) or 2+ (standard Ltda) |
| Shareholder liability | Limited to the value of their quota shares |
| Minimum capital | No statutory minimum (set in articles of association) |
| Foreign ownership | 100% permitted in most sectors |
| Governance | Simple — managed by one or more administrators (gerentes) |
| Accounting | Full bookkeeping required |
| Best for | Small to mid-size trading companies |

### SA — Sociedade Anônima (Corporation)

A joint-stock company used for larger businesses or those that intend to raise capital from multiple investors.

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Minimum shareholders | 2 |
| Share transfers | Freely transferable (publicly traded or closed) |
| Governance | Requires a Board of Directors (for public SA) |
| Complexity | Higher — mandatory annual audits for larger SAs, more filings |
| Best for | Companies planning to raise capital, list on a stock exchange, or have complex ownership structures |

### Recommendation for a Small Trading Company

**Use an Ltda (SLU if sole owner, standard Ltda if two or more owners).** It offers:
- Simplest governance structure
- Full foreign ownership without complexity
- No minimum capital requirement
- Lower compliance costs than SA
- Flexibility to add partners later

## 2. Requirements for Foreign Nationals

### Can a Japanese Person Own a Brazilian Company?

Yes. Brazil permits 100% foreign ownership of an Ltda in most commercial sectors including import/export trading. There is no requirement to have a Brazilian partner.

### Two Pathways: Resident vs. Non-Resident

#### Pathway A: Non-Resident Owner (Operating from Japan)

You can own a Brazilian company while living in Japan. This is legally possible but requires additional administrative infrastructure:

- **CPF (Cadastro de Pessoas Físicas):** Brazil's individual taxpayer ID. Any foreigner — including non-residents — can obtain a CPF. Apply online through the Receita Federal portal or at a Brazilian consulate in Japan. Processing takes 1–2 weeks. This is the first and most critical document.
- **Power of Attorney (Procuração):** You must appoint a Brazilian-resident attorney-in-fact (procurador) with full powers to act on your behalf in all company matters: signing documents, representing the company before government authorities, opening bank accounts, and so on. The power of attorney must be:
  - Signed by you in Japan
  - Apostilled under the Hague Apostille Convention (Brazil and Japan are both signatories)
  - Translated into Portuguese by a sworn translator (tradutor juramentado) registered in Brazil
- **Resident Administrator:** The Junta Comercial (Board of Trade) requires that the company have an administrator who resides in Brazil. This can be your attorney-in-fact, a hired local manager, or a professional director service.
- **Central Bank Registration:** Foreign investments of USD 100,000 or more must be registered with the Banco Central do Brasil through the Sistema SCE-IED (Sistema de Controle de Capitais Estrangeiros — Investimentos Estrangeiros Diretos).

#### Pathway B: Resident Owner (Living in Brazil)

If you relocate to Brazil, additional documents are required:

- **CRNM (Carteira de Registro Nacional Migratório):** Formerly called RNE (Registro Nacional de Estrangeiro). This is Brazil's foreigner registration card, issued by the Federal Police after you obtain a qualifying visa. It proves your legal residency.
- **Investor Visa (VIPER):** The most relevant visa for a Japanese business owner. Requirements:
  - Invest at least R$500,000 in a new or existing Brazilian company, OR
  - Invest R$150,000 in priority sectors (technology, innovation, startups)
  - Submit a business plan
  - Clean criminal record
  - This grants immediate permanent residency (not just temporary)
- **VITEM V (Temporary Work Visa):** For directors/administrators actively managing the company. Requires a work permit from the Ministry of Labor.
- **Path to Citizenship:** After 4 years of legal residency, you may apply for Brazilian citizenship.

#### Practical Note for a Small Trading Operation Starting from Japan

Most small foreign-owned trading companies in Brazil begin with Pathway A (non-resident ownership) until the business generates sufficient revenue to justify relocation. The key cost is finding a reliable resident administrator/attorney-in-fact. Professional service firms in Brazil offer this service for approximately R$500–R$1,500/month.

## 3. Step-by-Step Company Registration

The entire process flows through the REDESIM (Rede Nacional para Simplificação do Registro e da Legalização de Empresas e Negócios) portal, which integrates most federal, state, and municipal registrations.

### Step 1: Obtain Your CPF

**Where:** Online via the Receita Federal website (gov.br/receitafederal) or at a Brazilian consulate in Japan.

**Documents needed:**
- Valid Japanese passport
- Proof of address in Japan

**Timeline:** 1–2 weeks
**Cost:** Free

### Step 2: Hire a Brazilian Accountant (Contador)

Do this before anything else. In Brazil, a licensed contador (CRC-registered accountant) must co-sign all company registration documents filed with the Junta Comercial. Without one, you cannot proceed. The accountant will also prepare your articles of association (Contrato Social) and handle REDESIM filings on your behalf.

**Cost:** R$300–R$800/month ongoing; some accountants include company formation in this fee or charge a one-time setup fee of R$500–R$2,000.

### Step 3: Choose a Company Name and CNAE Code

- Check name availability via the Junta Comercial of your state
- Choose the appropriate CNAE (Classificação Nacional de Atividades Econômicas) codes that cover your business activities. For a trading company doing import and export, the relevant codes include:
  - **4612-5/00** — Representatives and agents in the trade of agricultural materials and livestock (for coffee)
  - **4613-3/00** — Representatives in the trade of timber, building materials, and hardware
  - **4679-6/99** — Trade of other non-specified products (can cover gemstones)
  - **4649-4/08** — Trade of articles for personal adornment (includes jewelry/gemstones)
  - **4646-0/01** — Trade of textile articles (could cover stationery)
  - **5150-7/00** — Trade of computer equipment and peripherals (for manga/books as media)
  - **4618-4/03** — Representatives in trade of editorial products and publications (for manga)
  - **6201-5/00** or export-specific CNAE codes for foreign trade intermediaries
  - **4611-7/00** — Representatives for agricultural raw materials including coffee

Your contador will help select the correct CNAE codes. A trading company typically registers 3–6 CNAE codes to cover all planned activities.

### Step 4: Prepare the Contrato Social (Articles of Association)

Your contador or lawyer drafts this document. It must contain:
- Company name and type (Ltda or SLU)
- Registered address in Brazil
- Business purpose (objeto social) linked to your CNAE codes
- Capital social (declared capital)
- Quota distribution among shareholders
- Name and CPF of each administrator
- Rules for decision-making and profit distribution
- Duration (usually indefinite)

This document must be in Portuguese.

### Step 5: Register with the Junta Comercial (State Board of Trade)

The Junta Comercial is the state-level commercial registry. Each Brazilian state has its own:
- São Paulo: JUCESP
- Rio de Janeiro: JUCERJA
- Minas Gerais: JUCEMG
- Espírito Santo: JUCEES (relevant if operating near gemstone mining regions)

**Process:** Submit the Contrato Social and supporting documents (digitally via REDESIM in most states).

**Timeline:** 5–20 business days (São Paulo is faster at 5–10 days; some states take longer)

**Cost:** R$1,000–R$2,000 in filing fees, varies by state

### Step 6: Obtain CNPJ (Federal Tax ID)

The CNPJ (Cadastro Nacional de Pessoas Jurídicas) is Brazil's corporate tax identification number. In most states, the CNPJ is issued automatically through REDESIM simultaneously with or immediately after Junta Comercial registration.

**Timeline:** Usually issued within 1–3 business days of Junta approval
**Cost:** Free

### Step 7: Obtain State Tax Registration (Inscrição Estadual)

Required for companies that trade physical goods (which your trading company will). This registers you with the SEFAZ (Secretaria da Fazenda Estadual) for ICMS (the state goods-and-services tax).

**Process:** Filed through REDESIM, or separately via the state SEFAZ portal.

**Timeline:** 5–15 business days
**Cost:** Free or minimal administrative fee

### Step 8: Obtain Municipal Business License (Alvará de Funcionamento)

Required to legally operate at your registered address. Filed with the Prefeitura (city hall) of your municipality.

**Process:** Submit CNPJ, Contrato Social, proof of address (lease agreement or property deed), and floor plan of premises.

**Timeline:** 15–30 business days
**Cost:** R$200–R$800 depending on municipality

### Step 9: Register for ISS (if providing services)

If your company charges service fees (e.g., brokerage or intermediary fees), you must also register with the municipal ISS (Imposto Sobre Serviços) system. This is often done simultaneously with the alvará application.

### Step 10: Register with Central Bank (if applicable)

If total foreign investment equals or exceeds USD 100,000, register via the SCE-IED system on the Banco Central do Brasil portal (www4.bcb.gov.br). This is a declaratory registration, not an approval process.

### Step 11: Obtain Digital Certificate (e-CNPJ)

Required to access government systems electronically, including RADAR and Siscomex. Purchase from a certified authority (Certisign, Serasa, Valid).

**Types:** A1 (software-based, valid 1 year) or A3 (hardware token, valid 3 years)
**Cost:** R$150–R$350

## 5. Minimum Capital Requirements

There is **no statutory minimum capital** for an Ltda or SLU in Brazil since the 2019 Economic Freedom Law.

However, practical considerations apply:

- The capital declared in your Contrato Social (Capital Social) should reflect the actual resources being contributed to the business. It does not need to be deposited in a bank immediately but represents the owner's commitment.
- For **RADAR registration** (import/export authorization), the Receita Federal evaluates your financial capacity. To obtain Radar Ilimitado (unlimited import operations), you need to demonstrate liquid assets or checking account balances exceeding USD 150,000 (approximately R$750,000–R$850,000 depending on exchange rate).
- For the **investor visa (VIPER)**, minimum investment of R$500,000 is required.
- A practical starting capital of **R$50,000–R$200,000** declared in the Contrato Social is common for small trading companies.

## 6. Address Requirements

Your company must have a registered address (sede) in Brazil. Options:

### Option 1: Physical Office (Sala Comercial)
Lease a commercial office space. This is the most straightforward option and raises no issues with any government authority.
- **Cost:** Highly variable — R$1,500–R$5,000/month in major cities
- Requires: IPTU (property tax) proof, lease contract

### Option 2: Virtual Office / Fiscal Address
Many companies use virtual office services for their registered address. This is legal and widely used in Brazil.
- **Cost:** R$150–R$500/month
- Provides: Registered address for CNPJ and alvará purposes, mail handling, meeting rooms on demand
- **Important:** The municipal alvará for the address must permit commercial activity. Confirm with the virtual office provider that their address is zoned for trading company activity.

### Option 3: Home Address
Some small companies use the owner's Brazilian residential address. Only viable if you have a Brazilian residence.

### Practical Note
If operating near gemstone regions (Minas Gerais), consider registering in Belo Horizonte or Teófilo Otoni. For coffee, São Paulo or Santos (Brazil's largest port) is convenient. For manga/stationery imports, São Paulo is the main entry point.

## 7. Brazilian Accountant (Contador) Requirements

### Is a Contador Mandatory?

**Yes, effectively mandatory.** Brazilian law requires that every company's accounting documents be signed and submitted by a licensed accountant (Contador) holding an active CRC (Conselho Regional de Contabilidade) registration. Without a contador, you cannot:
- File your monthly DAS (tax payment slip) or DCTF
- Submit annual SPED (Sistema Público de Escrituração Digital) filings
- Access certain government portals
- Maintain legal compliance

### What a Contador Does for Your Company

- Prepares and files monthly bookkeeping (escrituração contábil)
- Calculates and submits monthly tax payments
- Prepares the annual ECD (Escrituração Contábil Digital) and ECF (Escrituração Contábil Fiscal)
- Files DCTF (Declaração de Débitos e Créditos Tributários Federais) monthly
- Manages payroll if you hire employees
- Advises on tax regime
- Co-signs company registration documents at the Junta Comercial

### Costs (2025–2026)

| Company Size / Activity | Monthly Fee (BRL) |
|---|---|
| Inactive / minimal activity | R$200–R$350 |
| Small trading company, Lucro Presumido | R$500–R$1,200 |
| Active import/export operations | R$800–R$2,000 |
| With employees (add per employee) | R$50–R$100/employee/month |

For a small trading company doing a moderate volume of import/export under Lucro Presumido, budget approximately **R$600–R$1,000/month** for accounting.

### Finding a Contador

- Use the CFC (Conselho Federal de Contabilidade) online registry to verify credentials
- Many contadores specializing in foreign trade (comércio exterior) are found in São Paulo, Santos, and Belo Horizonte
- Ask your Brazilian lawyer for a referral
- Online accounting services (e.g., Contabilizei, ContaAzul's accounting network) exist but may have limited expertise in comércio exterior

## 8. RADAR Registration for Import/Export

### What is RADAR?

RADAR (Registro e Rastreamento da Atuação dos Intervenientes Aduaneiros) is the authorization issued by the Receita Federal do Brasil that grants a company the right to operate in foreign trade through Siscomex. Without RADAR, you cannot legally import or export goods in Brazil. It is distinct from the CNPJ — having a CNPJ does not automatically grant RADAR access.

### Three RADAR Modalities

| Modality | Import Limit (per 6 months) | Export Limit | Who it's for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Expresso** | USD 50,000 | Unlimited | New companies, exporters only, or companies with small import needs |
| **Limitado** | USD 50,000–USD 150,000 | Unlimited | Companies with proven financial capacity above base Expresso threshold |
| **Ilimitado** | No limit | Unlimited | Companies demonstrating liquid assets exceeding USD 150,000 |

**For export operations (coffee, gemstones), all three modalities have no pre-set value limit.** The limit only applies to imports (manga, stationery). A new trading company should initially apply for **Radar Expresso** or **Radar Limitado**, then upgrade to Ilimitado as the business grows.

### RADAR Application Process

1. **Prerequisites:** Valid CNPJ, e-CNPJ digital certificate, and at least 3–6 months of operational history (some states require demonstrated financial activity)
2. **Submit application** via the Receita Federal's Portal Único do Comércio Exterior (PUCOMEX) or through the Siscomex portal (siscomex.gov.br)
3. **Documents required:**
   - CNPJ and Contrato Social
   - Last 3 months of bank statements (showing financial capacity)
   - Financial statements or income tax return (if available)
   - Evidence of equity/working capital (for Limitado/Ilimitado)
   - Power of attorney (if submitting through a representative)
4. **Receita Federal review:** The RFB analyzes financial capacity, fiscal regularity, and company profile
5. **Timeline:** 15–60 business days (Expresso is faster; Ilimitado takes longer due to scrutiny)

### Key Point: Radar Ilimitado Requirements

To obtain Ilimitado, the Receita Federal looks for:
- Liquid assets (cash in bank accounts or financial investments) exceeding USD 150,000
- Clean fiscal record (Certidão Negativa de Débitos — CND — from Receita Federal and PGFN)
- Consistent accounting and tax compliance history

A new company without significant assets will start at Expresso or Limitado and upgrade over time.

### RADAR Maintenance

RADAR authorization can be suspended if:
- The company accumulates tax debts
- Fiscal irregularities are detected
- The company is inactive for an extended period

Maintain tax compliance rigorously to avoid suspension.

## 9. Siscomex Registration

### What is Siscomex?

Siscomex (Sistema Integrado de Comércio Exterior) is the Brazilian government's integrated foreign trade management system. Once RADAR is granted, your company automatically gains access to Siscomex to register import and export operations.

### Key Siscomex Documents

**For Exports:**
- **RE (Registro de Exportação):** Required for exports exceeding USD 50,000. Declares commercial, financial, exchange, and tax information about the export operation.
- **DSE (Declaração Simplificada de Exportação):** For shipments under USD 50,000. Simplified version.
- **DU-E (Declaração Única de Exportação):** The modern unified export declaration that has progressively replaced older forms since 2017.

**For Imports:**
- **LI (Licença de Importação):** Required for controlled goods (agricultural products, certain minerals). Applied for before the goods are shipped.
- **DUIMP (Declaração Única de Importação):** The new unified import declaration replacing the old DI (Declaração de Importação). Being progressively rolled out throughout 2025.
- **DI (Declaração de Importação):** Still used for some categories during the DUIMP transition period.

### Access

Access Siscomex via:
- Portal Único do Comércio Exterior: portalunico.mdic.gov.br
- Requires your e-CNPJ digital certificate

## 10. Cecafé Registration for Coffee Export

### What is Cecafé?

Cecafé (Conselho dos Exportadores de Café do Brasil) is a private industry council representing Brazilian coffee exporters. It is not a government registry but rather a trade association whose members represent approximately 96% of Brazilian coffee exports.

### Is Cecafé Membership Mandatory?

**No.** Cecafé membership is voluntary. However, being a member provides significant practical benefits:
- Access to trade statistics and market intelligence
- Participation in trade missions and international coffee fairs
- Networking with buyers and logistics providers
- Credibility with international buyers who recognize the Cecafé brand

### What IS Mandatory for Coffee Export

1. **RADAR/Siscomex authorization** (see sections 8–9 above)
2. **MAPA Registration via SIPEAGRO:** The Ministry of Agriculture (MAPA) requires coffee exporters to register through SIPEAGRO (Sistema de Informações Gerenciais do MAPA). This registration certifies your establishment as authorized to handle and export agricultural products.
3. **VIGIAGRO Compliance:** VIGIAGRO (Vigilância Agropecuária Internacional) is MAPA's agricultural surveillance division. It inspects coffee at the port of exit and issues the phytosanitary certificate required by the importing country (Japan's quarantine authority requires this).
4. **Phytosanitary Certificate (Certificado Fitossanitário):** Issued by MAPA/VIGIAGRO for each shipment. Required by Japan's Plant Protection Law. Apply per shipment at the VIGIAGRO office at your port of export.
5. **ICO Certificate of Origin:** The International Coffee Organization (ICO) certificate of origin is required for exports to ICO member countries (Japan is a member). In Brazil, these are issued through the MDIC (Ministério do Desenvolvimento, Indústria, Comércio e Serviços).
6. **Export Registration (RE) in Siscomex:** For each shipment.

### Coffee Export Process Summary

1. Source and purchase coffee from a certified farm or cooperative
2. Ensure coffee is properly processed, graded, and packaged
3. Register the export operation in Siscomex (RE or DU-E)
4. Request VIGIAGRO inspection and obtain phytosanitary certificate
5. Obtain ICO certificate of origin from MDIC
6. Arrange shipping through Santos, Vitória, or another approved port
7. File all export documentation (commercial invoice, packing list, bill of lading, RE, phytosanitary certificate, ICO certificate)

### Japan Import Requirements for Coffee

When exporting to Japan, also ensure:
- Compliance with Japan's Food Sanitation Law (食品衛生法) for coffee as a food product
- Maximum residue limits for pesticides as set by the Japanese Ministry of Health
- Labeling in Japanese (required for retail; not required for bulk/green coffee)

## 11. IBGM and ANM Requirements for Gemstone Export

### Role of IBGM

IBGM (Instituto Brasileiro de Gemas e Metais Preciosos) is a private, non-profit industry association representing the gems and precious metals supply chain. Like Cecafé for coffee, IBGM membership is **voluntary** but beneficial for:
- Industry connections and credibility
- Access to trade show participation (e.g., Brazil Gems & Jewelry)
- Market intelligence
- Advocacy on export regulations

### Key Regulatory Body: ANM

The ANM (Agência Nacional de Mineração), created in 2017 to replace the DNPM, is the federal body that regulates mining activities in Brazil. For a trading company that **buys already-extracted gemstones** (not a mining company), the ANM requirements are simpler:

#### If You Are Only Trading (Not Mining)

A trading company that purchases gemstones from licensed miners, cooperatives, or garimpeiros (artisanal miners) for export does not need an ANM mining license itself. However:

- You must **buy from sellers who have legitimate ANM-licensed operations.** Purchasing from illegal garimpos (illegal mines) is a serious criminal offense in Brazil.
- The ANM has issued resolutions specifically targeting money laundering and terrorism financing in precious metals and gemstone transactions, imposing due diligence obligations on buyers.
- **Keep documentation** of each purchase: origin certificate, seller's ANM license or garimpeiro permit, invoice, and provenance records.

#### CFEM (Compensação Financeira pela Exploração Mineral) — Royalties

CFEM is a mining royalty paid to the government by mining operations. As a trader (not miner), you do not pay CFEM. Your supplier (the miner) pays it. However, ensure your suppliers are compliant — non-payment of CFEM can result in seized goods at customs.

#### Gemstone Export Process

1. Purchase gemstones from licensed Brazilian miners/dealers with proper documentation
2. Obtain an export valuation from a certified gemologist or recognized institution (for customs purposes)
3. Register the export in Siscomex (RE or DU-E)
4. For rough diamonds specifically: obtain a **Kimberley Process Certificate (KPC)** from the ANM before export. Brazil is a KPC signatory. The KPC proves the diamond was not conflict-sourced.
5. For other gemstones (aquamarine, Imperial Topaz, Alexandrite, tourmaline, etc.): no Kimberley Process requirement, but provenance documentation is still expected
6. Submit export documentation: commercial invoice, packing list, gemological certificate (from GIA, AGL, or reputable lab), RE from Siscomex, proof of purchase chain
7. Ship via secure carrier (insured) — DHL, Brink's, Malca-Amit, or Segurança Total for high-value stones

#### Japan Import Requirements for Gemstones

- Gemstones are not subject to food safety or quarantine rules in Japan
- Japan imposes import duty of approximately 4% on cut gemstones (HS Chapter 71)
- Gemological laboratory certificates (GIA, AGL, GRS, or Japanese lab GGTL) strongly preferred by Japanese buyers
- No import license required for gemstones into Japan, but declare value accurately to Japanese customs

## 12. Opening a Business Bank Account

### Requirements

A Brazilian corporate bank account requires:
- Valid CNPJ
- Contrato Social (articles of association)
- e-CNPJ digital certificate
- CPF and identity documents for all administrators/signatories
- Proof of registered address
- Complete corporate ownership structure (UBO chart — identifying the ultimate beneficial owner up to the individual level)
- For foreign shareholders: apostilled, translated personal documents
- Power of attorney if administrator is acting on behalf of non-resident shareholder

### Recommended Banks for Foreign-Owned Trading Companies

**Traditional Banks (Better for Foreign Exchange Operations)**

| Bank | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Itaú Unibanco** | Largest private bank; experienced with foreign-owned companies; strong FX desk |
| **Banco do Brasil** | State bank; extensive network; good for agricultural trade (coffee) |
| **Bradesco** | Large network; handles foreign-owned entities; competitive FX rates |
| **Santander Brasil** | International bank; often smoother onboarding for foreign-owned companies due to parent company protocols |

**Digital Banks (Faster Onboarding, Limited FX)**

| Bank | Notes |
|---|---|
| **C6 Bank** | Business accounts available; modern interface; limited international trade features |
| **Nubank (NuEmpresa)** | Growing business banking; limited for trade finance |
| **Inter Empresas** | Digital business banking; limited for complex FX operations |

**Recommendation:** Use a traditional bank for your primary operating account, given the need for regular foreign exchange contracts (Fechamento de Câmbio) for international payments to Japan and receipt of export proceeds. Itaú or Santander are particularly well-suited.

### Foreign Exchange Requirements

Brazil has strict foreign exchange controls. Every international payment requires:
- A **Contrato de Câmbio (FX contract)** signed with your bank
- Declaration to the Banco Central for each transaction
- For export proceeds: funds must be received into Brazil within the legally required timeframe (currently 750 days from shipment for exports)
- For import payments: must be paid via official banking channels with proper documentation

### Timeline to Open Account

- Traditional banks: 4–12 weeks (extensive KYC due diligence on foreign-owned entities)
- In-person visit required (your resident administrator must attend in person)
- Some banks require the foreign shareholder to appear in person at least once

## 13. Timeline from Start to First Trade Operation

The following assumes you begin from Japan with no Brazilian presence.

| Phase | Activities | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| **Phase 1: Pre-Setup (Japan-based)** | Obtain CPF (apply online); engage Brazilian lawyer and contador; prepare and apostille Power of Attorney; source Japan-based documents | 4–6 weeks |
| **Phase 2: Company Registration** | Submit Contrato Social to Junta Comercial; obtain CNPJ; state tax registration; municipal alvará | 4–8 weeks |
| **Phase 3: Post-Registration Setup** | Open bank account; obtain e-CNPJ digital certificate; register with Central Bank (if applicable) | 4–12 weeks |
| **Phase 4: Trade Authorizations** | Apply for RADAR; MAPA/SIPEAGRO registration for coffee; obtain Siscomex access | 4–8 weeks |
| **Phase 5: First Trade Operation** | Source goods; register export/import in Siscomex; arrange logistics; obtain certificates | 2–4 weeks per shipment |
| **TOTAL** | | **4–9 months** |

### Realistic Timeline

- **Optimistic case (São Paulo, experienced team):** 4–5 months to first trade
- **Realistic case:** 6–8 months
- **Conservative case (delays in banking, RADAR, or municipal license):** 9–12 months

The biggest variable is usually the **bank account opening** (4–12 weeks) and **RADAR authorization** (2–8 weeks after all prerequisites are met).

## 15. Tax Regime Options

### Overview

Brazil has three main tax regimes for corporate entities. The choice significantly affects your effective tax rate and compliance burden.

### Simples Nacional

**Annual revenue limit:** Up to R$4.8 million

**Who qualifies:** Micro and small businesses whose shareholders are **individual persons** (not corporate entities) and who are either Brazilian or hold **permanent Brazilian residency**. As a Japanese non-resident or temporary resident, you likely cannot access Simples Nacional initially.

**Why it matters:** Even if you obtain a permanent investor visa (VIPER), confirm with your contador whether your specific company structure qualifies. An SLU (single-member Ltda) owned by a foreign national with permanent residency can potentially qualify.

**Tax rates:** 4%–22.9% of gross revenue (progressive tables, divided by sector — Annex I for commerce/trade is most relevant for your import/export activity)

**For a trading company under Annex I (commerce):**
- Revenue up to R$180,000/year: 4%
- R$180,001–R$360,000: 7.3%
- R$360,001–R$720,000: 9.5%
- R$720,001–R$1.8M: 10.7%
- R$1.8M–R$3.6M: 14.3%
- R$3.6M–R$4.8M: 19%

**Advantage:** Huge simplification — one monthly payment (DAS) replaces IRPJ, CSLL, PIS, COFINS, ICMS, ISS, and INSS (CPP).

### Lucro Presumido (Presumed Profit)

**Annual revenue limit:** Up to R$78 million

**Who qualifies:** Most companies not required to use Lucro Real; no residency restrictions on shareholders.

**This is the most practical regime for a small foreign-owned trading company.**

**How it works:**
- The government assumes (presumes) a profit margin and calculates taxes on that
- For **commercial trading companies (buying and selling goods):** Presumed profit margin = **8% of gross revenue** for IRPJ purposes, 12% for CSLL
- Taxes calculated on that presumed profit:
  - IRPJ: 15% (+ 10% surtax on profits above R$60,000/quarter = ~R$240,000/year)
  - CSLL: 9%
  - PIS: 0.65% of gross revenue
  - COFINS: 3% of gross revenue

**Effective total tax rate on gross revenue for a trading company under Lucro Presumido:**
- Approximately 5.93%–6.73% of gross revenue (combining IRPJ, CSLL, PIS, COFINS on typical margins)
- Plus ICMS (state), which varies by state and product (typically 7%–18% on goods sold within Brazil — but exports are ICMS-exempt)
- Plus ISS (municipal) if providing services (usually 2%–5%)

**Key advantage for exporters:** Coffee and gemstone exports are **exempt from ICMS, PIS, and COFINS** under Brazilian export incentive rules. This significantly reduces the tax burden on the export side.

### Lucro Real (Actual Profit)

**Who it applies to:** Mandatory for companies with revenue above R$78 million/year, or companies in certain financial sectors. Optional for all others.

**How it works:** Taxes calculated on actual booked net profit after deductions.

**Tax rates:**
- IRPJ: 15% (+ 10% surtax on quarterly profit above R$60,000)
- CSLL: 9%
- PIS: 1.65% of revenue (non-cumulative regime, but with credits available)
- COFINS: 7.6% of revenue (non-cumulative, with credits available)

**When Lucro Real is better:** When your profit margins are very low (e.g., thin-margin bulk commodity trading), or when you have large deductible expenses. For a startup with early-stage losses, Lucro Real allows you to offset losses against future profits.

### Recommendation for This Trading Company

| Scenario | Recommended Regime |
|---|---|
| Foreign owner with permanent visa, revenue < R$4.8M | **Simples Nacional** (if eligible — lowest tax and simplest compliance) |
| Foreign owner without permanent visa, revenue < R$78M | **Lucro Presumido** (standard choice, manageable compliance) |
| Very thin margins on bulk coffee trading | **Lucro Real** (deduct actual costs) |

**For most startups in this situation: begin with Lucro Presumido.** It is well-understood by contadores specializing in foreign trade, it has no investor residency requirements, and the effective rate is reasonable for a trading company. Reassess at year 2–3 based on actual revenue and margin profile.

### Tax Reform Note (2025–2033)

Brazil is undergoing a major tax reform that will progressively replace PIS, COFINS, ICMS, and ISS with three new taxes: **IBS** (Imposto sobre Bens e Serviços), **CBS** (Contribuição sobre Bens e Serviços), and **IS** (Imposto Seletivo — Selective Tax). The transition runs 2026–2033. Export exemptions are preserved in the new system (IBS/CBS will not apply to exports). Your contador must stay current with reform implementation timelines.

## 18. Import Side: Manga and Stationery from Japan

This section addresses importing goods into Brazil (the reverse direction of the export operations).

### Import Process for Cultural Goods (Manga)

**Manga are classified under:**
- NCM 4901.99.00 — Printed books (other)
- NCM 4901.10.00 — Printed books in single sheets
- NCM 4902.90.00 — Newspapers and periodicals

**Brazilian import duties on manga (books):**
- Books are **exempt from federal import taxes (II, IPI, PIS/COFINS)** under the Brazilian Constitution's immunity for books and periodicals
- ICMS: Many states also exempt books from ICMS, but verify with your state's SEFAZ
- This makes manga imports **extremely tax-efficient** in Brazil

**Practical considerations:**
- ANVISA review not required (books are not food or health products)
- INMETRO certification not required (books are not consumer safety products under mandatory certification)
- Intellectual property: Ensure you have distribution rights for the specific manga titles in Brazil. Many manga are licensed to Brazilian publishers (Panini, JBC, Editora Devir). Importing for resale titles that have exclusive licensees in Brazil creates legal exposure.

### Import Process for Stationery from Japan

**Stationery classified under:**
- NCM 8210.00.00 — Hand tools for writing/drawing
- NCM 9608.10.00 — Ballpoint pens
- NCM 9608.20.00 — Felt-tip pens and other fiber/porous-tipped pens
- NCM 4820.10.20 — Exercise books
- NCM 3926.10.00 — Office or school supplies of plastics

**Brazilian import taxes on stationery:**
- II (Imposto de Importação): typically 14%–20% depending on NCM code
- IPI (Industrialized Products Tax): 0%–15%
- PIS/COFINS imports: 9.25% combined (approximately)
- ICMS: varies by state (typically 12%–18%)
- Effective total tax burden on imports into Brazil is often 50%–80% of CIF (cost+insurance+freight) value

**INMETRO:** Some stationery categories require INMETRO certification before import. Writing instruments and school supplies may fall under INMETRO's compulsory certification programs (OCP). Confirm per NCM code with an import specialist.

**ANVISA:** Ink products (pens, markers) may require ANVISA review if they could be considered health-related products. Check per product.

### RADAR for Imports

For importing manga and stationery, the semi-annual value limits of your RADAR modality apply. If total imports exceed USD 50,000/6 months, you need at minimum Radar Limitado. For established import operations, pursue Radar Ilimitado.

## Summary: Recommended Action Sequence

1. **Engage a Brazilian lawyer and contador** with foreign trade (comércio exterior) experience immediately. This is the single most important first step.
2. **Obtain your CPF** — apply online at gov.br/receitafederal or at the Brazilian consulate in Japan.
3. **Prepare Power of Attorney** — your lawyer drafts it, you sign in Japan, apostille it, send it to Brazil with certified translation.
4. **Set up a virtual office address** in São Paulo or another major city.
5. **Register the Ltda (SLU)** via your contador through REDESIM.
6. **Obtain CNPJ, state registration, and alvará** — your contador handles this.
7. **Open a bank account** — your resident administrator attends in person. Use Itaú or Santander.
8. **Obtain e-CNPJ digital certificate.**
9. **Apply for RADAR** (start with Expresso or Limitado).
10. **Register with MAPA/SIPEAGRO** if exporting coffee.
11. **Source your first goods** and execute your first trade.

**Total realistic setup period: 6–8 months.**
**Total Year-One budget (excluding goods): approximately R$50,000–R$90,000.**
