# Brazilian Specialty Coffee Export to Japan — Comprehensive Guide

*Research current as of April 2026. Prices in USD, BRL, and JPY where available.*

---

## 1. Japanese Coffee Market Overview

### Market Size

Japan is the world's third-largest coffee market by volume and one of the highest-value per-capita markets in the world. The total Japan coffee market generated approximately **USD 21.3 billion** in revenue in 2024 and is projected to reach **USD 30.3 billion by 2030** (CAGR 6.1%). The specialty coffee segment is growing faster, at an estimated **CAGR of 10.4% through 2033**, making it the single most dynamic sector within the broader market.

Japan ranks consistently among the **top five destination markets** for Brazilian coffee exports alongside the United States, Germany, Italy, and Belgium. In January–September 2025, Japan received **1.891 million 60-kg bags** from Brazil, up 15% year-on-year.

### Consumer Preferences

**Pour-over / drip culture dominates specialty.** Japanese consumers are deeply familiar with manual brewing: V60, Chemex, Kalita Wave, and siphon are standard equipment in specialty cafes. This creates strong demand for coffees with clarity of flavor and nuanced aromatics — qualities that reward bright-to-medium acidity and clean processing.

**Light-to-medium roast preference is growing.** Japanese third-wave roasters are increasingly roasting light, revealing origin character. This benefits Brazilian coffees that historically were sold as espresso/dark-roast bases — producers who can achieve 85+ SCA scores with structured sweetness and fruit-forward naturals are now accessing a premium tier.

**Single-origin and traceability matter.** Informed Tokyo buyers want farm name, variety, altitude, and processing method on the bag. Lot-level traceability (down to the specific farm or cooperative lot) commands price premiums.

**Retail price tolerance is high.** Japanese specialty consumers pay:
- Entry specialty (chain/Blue Bottle Japan style): ¥1,500–2,500 per 200g (approximately ¥7,500–12,500/kg)
- Mid-tier independent roaster: ¥2,000–4,000 per 100g (approximately ¥20,000–40,000/kg)
- Premium/competition-grade: ¥4,000–10,000+ per 100g

### Key Market Facts for Exporters

- Japanese yen weakness (¥148–155/USD range in 2025–2026) increases costs for importers, but they continue to pay premiums for top-quality lots — margin pressure falls most heavily on commodity-grade imports
- Sustainability, ethical sourcing, and organic certification are increasingly table stakes for positioning in specialty channels
- Japan is a **top buyer of Cup of Excellence auction lots** — regularly one of the two or three largest purchasing countries by bag count
- Seasonal limited releases perform extremely well in Japan; treating each harvest as a distinct product with harvest year labeling matches consumer expectations

## 2. Best Brazilian Coffee Regions for Japan Export

### Tier 1 — Highest Premium Potential

#### Chapada Diamantina (Bahia)
- **Altitude:** 800–1,400m, some farms exceeding 1,400m
- **Why it suits Japan:** Flavor profiles are the most "African-adjacent" of Brazilian regions — bright citrus, tropical fruit, wine-like complexity, and lighter body. Japanese specialty buyers who love Ethiopian and Kenyan coffees are drawn to Chapada for its atypical Brazil profile.
- **Processing:** Both natural and washed, with washed lots being especially unusual for Brazil and commanding strong premiums in Japan
- **SCA range:** 84–89+ for best farms
- **Key caveat:** Smaller volume, less logistics infrastructure than Minas Gerais; works best for micro-lot direct trade

#### Cerrado Mineiro (Minas Gerais)
- **Altitude:** 800–1,300m
- **Status:** Brazil's first Designation of Origin (DO) for coffee (full DO since 2013)
- **Why it suits Japan:** The DO certification is instantly legible to Japanese specialty buyers who understand GI labeling from wine. Consistent climate with well-defined dry season enables predictable processing.
- **Flavor:** Chocolate, hazelnut, caramel, dried fruit on naturals; cleaner with mild citrus and honey on semi-washed
- **Processing:** Predominantly natural and pulped natural (honey); washed lots emerging from premium farms
- **SCA range:** 82–88 for specialty lots; exceptional farms reach 88–90
- **Volume:** High — can fulfill larger roaster commitments; good for both spot and annual contract buyers

#### Sul de Minas (Minas Gerais)
- **Altitude:** Averaging 950m; top farms at 1,000–1,200m
- **Volume:** Approximately 30% of Brazil's total output; the most commercially important region
- **Why it suits Japan:** Classic "Brazilian" profile that forms the base of many Japanese espresso blends — smooth, full body, low-to-mild acidity, chocolate and nut. Major Japanese roasters (Maruyama, Sarutahiko, various Kyoto/Osaka independents) use Sul de Minas as a blend anchor.
- **Processing:** Natural and pulped natural dominant
- **SCA range:** 80–86 typical; exceptional farms reach 87–88
- **Best use case:** Blend bases, espresso-forward products, volume commitments

### Tier 2 — Strong Specialty Potential

#### Mogiana (São Paulo / Minas Gerais border)
- **Altitude:** 800–1,100m
- **Flavor:** Full body, low acidity, caramel, brown sugar, stone fruit on naturals
- **Why it suits Japan:** Well-known name with Japanese roasters; reliable quality; works for both espresso and drip
- **SCA range:** 82–87

#### Serra da Mantiqueira / Mantiqueira de Minas
- **Altitude:** 1,000–1,400m — among Brazil's highest coffee zones
- **Flavor:** Elegant sweetness, delicate florals, stone fruit, clean finish; more complexity and acidity than other Minas regions
- **Why it suits Japan:** The altitude-driven complexity appeals to Japan's growing light-roast pour-over segment. Multiple Cup of Excellence winners have come from this micro-region.
- **SCA range:** 84–91 for top farms
- **Note:** This sub-region deserves separate identification from Sul de Minas in marketing materials — Japanese buyers recognize the distinction

## 3. Varieties and Cultivars That Command Premium Prices in Japan

### Yellow Bourbon (Bourbon Amarelo)
- Brazil's most prized heritage variety for specialty
- Lower yield, higher cup quality, naturally sweet with stone fruit and caramel notes
- In the 2017 Cup of Excellence Brazil, Yellow Bourbon samples scored 92.33 and sold for **USD $126/lb** at auction
- Japanese buyers specifically seek Yellow Bourbon as a named cultivar; it should be labeled explicitly on export documentation and retail bags
- Available mainly in Sul de Minas, Mogiana, Mantiqueira de Minas

### Red Bourbon
- Similar premium profile to Yellow Bourbon; slightly more herbaceous
- Less documented in Japan marketing than Yellow Bourbon but respected in specialty circles

### Catuai (Red and Yellow)
- The most widely planted Brazilian variety; workhorse of production
- High-quality Catuai lots from elevated farms (above 1,000m) can reach 84–86 SCA and are competitive in the Japan mid-specialty tier
- Not a premium cultivar name per se, but well-made Catuai sells well as a transparent blend component or entry-level single origin

### Mundo Novo
- Classic Brazilian cultivar; good body and sweetness; less sought after by connoisseurs than Bourbon
- Relevant for blends and mid-tier commitments

### Acaiá
- Less internationally known; large bean, good cup potential, niche interest from Japanese buyers who seek unusual cultivars

### Geisha / Gesha
- Historically associated with Panama and Colombia, but Brazilian Geisha plantings are growing (notably in Cerrado Mineiro and Minas Gerais)
- Brazilian Geisha at competition level sells for **USD $30–100+/lb** green; cup of Geisha-based coffee at Tokyo specialty shops fetches ¥800–2,000+ per serving
- Still a small and emerging category from Brazil, but upside is significant; Japanese buyers will pay strongly for verified Brazilian Geisha with 87+ scores

### Icatu and Obatã
- Hybrids with Timor Hybrid genetics; disease-resistant
- Can produce 84+ specialty cups but are not cultivar names that excite Japanese buyers
- Best positioned as quality-focused commodity lots

### Priority Hierarchy for Japan Export

1. Yellow Bourbon (highest premium, most recognized)
2. Geisha/Gesha (highest absolute price ceiling, growing demand)
3. Red Bourbon (solid premium)
4. Well-made Catuai from high altitude with documented score
5. Mundo Novo (blend use, volume)

## 4. Processing Methods Japanese Buyers Prefer

### Natural (Dry Process) — Brazil's Dominant Method
- **Preferred for:** Espresso blends, full-body drip offerings, seasonal fruit-forward limited releases
- **Flavor:** Heavy body, intense sweetness, dried fruit, berries, low acidity
- **Brazilian dominance:** The vast majority of Brazilian specialty coffee is naturally processed, which is a competitive advantage — Ethiopian-style naturals are popular in Japan but Ethiopian supply is inconsistent; well-made Brazilian naturals offer quality with supply reliability
- **Japanese buyer notes:** Onibus Coffee Tokyo explicitly uses natural Brazil as its espresso blend base; Fuglen sources naturals for their Japanese operations through Nordic Approach

### Honey (Pulped Natural) — Brazil's Semi-Dry
- **Preferred for:** The growing third-wave drip pour-over segment seeking balance between natural sweetness and washed clarity
- **Flavor:** Sweet, medium body, stone fruit, caramel, more structured than full natural
- **Japan fit:** Very strong. Japanese pour-over consumers appreciate the complexity without the ferment-forward intensity of naturals. Yellow Honey and Red Honey processed Bourbons from Cerrado Mineiro and Mantiqueira de Minas perform exceptionally well
- **Practical note:** Honey processing in Brazil requires careful QC in drying — inconsistent honey lots with fermentation defects are the main failure mode that Japanese buyers reject

### Washed (Wet Process) — Rare from Brazil, High Premium
- **Preferred for:** Premium pour-over and competition use; buyers who want brightness and terroir clarity
- **Flavor:** Clean, bright, higher acidity, origin-transparent, lighter body
- **Japan fit:** Extremely high if quality is there. Japan imports a large volume of washed Ethiopian and Kenyan; a well-made washed Brazilian from Chapada Diamantina or Mantiqueira can differentiate by showing what Brazil's terroir tastes like without the expected "Brazilian" profile
- **Price premium:** Washed Brazilian lots command 20–40% premium over equivalent natural lots from the same farm due to rarity and novelty
- **Volume caveat:** Washed Brazilian is a micro-lot category; do not attempt to fulfill large contracts with washed Brazil

### Processing Summary

| Method | Japan Segment | Price Premium vs. Baseline | Availability |
|---|---|---|---|
| Natural | Espresso, blends, drip | Baseline | High volume |
| Honey (Yellow/Red) | Pour-over, drip specialty | +10–25% | Moderate |
| Washed | Premium pour-over, competition | +20–40% | Low (micro-lot) |
| Anaerobic / Experimental | Competition, limited release | +50–200% | Very limited |

## 5. Grading and Certification That Increases Japan Selling Price

### SCA Score Thresholds

The Specialty Coffee Association (SCA) scoring system is the global standard. For Japan export:

| SCA Score | Classification | Commercial Position | Typical Green Price (FOB, 2024–2025) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 80.0–83.9 | Specialty (entry) | Mid-market blends | USD $2.93–4.50/lb ($6.45–9.90/kg) |
| 84.0–86.9 | Excellent specialty | Single origin, quality roasters | USD $4.18–7.00/lb ($9.21–15.43/kg) |
| 87.0–89.9 | Outstanding | Premium single origin, top Japanese roasters | USD $7.00–15.00/lb ($15.43–33.07/kg) |
| 90.0+ | World class / competition | Competition lots, COE auction | USD $15.00–100.00+/lb |

Note: Prices above are FOB Brazil medians. Japan-specific prices are typically 10–25% higher than the global median due to Japan's premium buyer base and yen-denominated cost of quality sourcing.

### Cup of Excellence (COE)

- The most prestigious specialty auction in the world for Brazil; Japan is consistently one of the top 2–3 buying countries
- In 2024, the highest bid for a Brazil COE lot reached **USD $13,902.63 per 60-kg bag** (~$232/kg or ~$105/lb)
- Any farm that wins or places in COE gains immediate credibility with Japanese specialty roasters — market the win in all communications, on bags, and at SCAJ
- Coffees must score 87+ in national rounds and pass international jury scoring
- Even placing in the top 30 (scoring above 87) is marketable in Japan as "COE Pre-Selected" or "National Finalist"

### Designation of Origin: Cerrado Mineiro
- Brazil's first and most recognized coffee DO; explicitly requires: 800m+ altitude, Arabica variety, member of certified cooperative/association, 80+ SCA, compliant with Brazilian agricultural law
- The DO seal is legible to Japanese buyers who understand geographic indication from wine and tea; use it on export documents and retail packaging
- Direct analog in Japanese minds: similar to AOC wine or designated sake regions

### JAS Organic Certification (Japan Agricultural Standards)
- Required to label coffee as "organic" in Japan — the word "organic" / オーガニック cannot appear on packaging without JAS certification
- JAS organic is enforced by Japan's MAFF (Ministry of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries)
- Brazilian farmers with USDA Organic certification can access the Japan market under the U.S.-Japan Organic Equivalence Arrangement (via TM-11 export certificate) — this is the most practical path for Brazilian producers who already have USDA Organic
- JAS organic green coffee commands a price premium of approximately **15–30% in Japan** over equivalent non-organic specialty lots
- Cost of JAS/USDA organic certification: BRL 5,000–15,000/year depending on farm size and certifying body; bodies operating in Brazil include IBD Certifications, Ecocert, and Kiwa BCS

### Rainforest Alliance / UTZ
- Increasingly table stakes for mid-market buyers (café chains, commercial roasters)
- Less impactful for pure specialty channels where relationship and cup score matter more
- Nonetheless, having Rainforest Alliance certification removes a barrier when approaching Japan's larger foodservice buyers (Starbucks Japan, Doutor, UCC)

### Fair Trade
- Recognized by Japanese consumers; has a dedicated retail market through cooperatives and organic specialty retailers (e.g., People Tree, organic supermarkets)
- Less meaningful to the top-tier third-wave roasters who prioritize direct trade pricing over Fair Trade label

### Roasted Coffee Retail in Japan (for margin context)

- Japanese specialty roasters typically charge retail customers:
  - ¥4,000–8,000/100g for COE or competition-grade lots
  - ¥1,500–3,000/100g for premium single-origin (87–89 SCA)
  - ¥800–1,500/100g for good specialty (84–86 SCA)
  - ¥500–800/100g for entry specialty and quality blends
- Roaster gross margin on green-to-roasted is typically 50–70%; they buy green at approximately 25–35% of their retail roasted price
- A roaster selling at ¥2,000/100g (¥20,000/kg roasted) would typically pay approximately ¥5,000–7,000/kg green (≈ $33–47/kg at ¥150/USD)

### Direct Importer Purchase Price (Reality Check)

Based on March 2025 data, Brazil's average green coffee export price **to Japan specifically** was **USD $6,839/ton ($6.84/kg)**, which reflects the blended average of all quality tiers. The specialty-specific average was **USD $425 per 60-kg bag ($7.08/kg)** for January–September 2025. These averages are dragged down by commercial-grade lots. Top-tier direct-trade specialty lots negotiate significantly higher — use the table above for segmented pricing.

## 8. How to Reach Japanese Buyers

### SCAJ World Specialty Coffee Conference and Exhibition

The single most important event for breaking into the Japan market.

- **Location:** Tokyo Big Sight, South Exhibition Halls
- **Dates:** Annually in October; **SCAJ 2026 is October 14–17, 2026**
- **Scale:** ~35,000 attendees, ~200 exhibitors; B2B-oriented
- **What happens:** Cupping competitions, trade floor, producer country pavilions, direct buyer-seller meetings
- **Cost to exhibit:** Varies; a shared pavilion booth through BSCA (Brazilian Specialty Coffee Association) or APEX-Brasil (government export promotion agency) is the most cost-effective entry — often subsidized

**Action steps:**
1. Contact BSCA (bsca.com.br) about coordinating a Brazilian specialty pavilion presence at SCAJ 2026
2. Contact APEX-Brasil (apexbrasil.com.br) — they fund producer participation in international trade shows, including SCAJ
3. Register as a visitor first if budget is limited; attend, cup, make contacts, and exhibit at a subsequent year

### Direct Outreach to Roasters

- **LinkedIn:** Most Japanese specialty coffee professionals have LinkedIn presence; direct message with a brief introduction, your farm/region, SCA score data, and a link to a cupping report
- **Email:** More effective if you have a mutual referral (e.g., "I met [X] at SCAJ and they suggested I reach out")
- **Sample sending:** Always offer to send a 250–500g green sample for cupping before asking for a purchase commitment; ship via courier (DHL/FedEx), include a cupping sheet with your SCA score, processing notes, and farm details

### Green Coffee Brokers and Intermediaries

Using a broker is the most reliable path for first-time exporters:

- **Algrano** (algrano.com) — direct trade digital platform connecting Brazilian farms with international roasters; Japanese roasters are active users
- **Covoya Coffee** — specialty green importer active in Japan channel
- **Nordic Approach** (nordicapproach.no) — if you want to reach Fuglen Tokyo and other Nordic-influenced roasters
- **Cafe Imports** (cafeimports.com) — U.S.-based specialty importer with Japan distribution; consigning lots to Cafe Imports gives access to their Japanese buyer network
- **Sucafina** (sucafina.com) — global specialty trader with Japan relationships; can handle full export logistics from Brazil

### Japan External Trade Organization (JETRO)

JETRO (jetro.go.jp) runs free matchmaking programs connecting foreign producers with Japanese buyers. JETRO São Paulo office is staffed by Japanese trade specialists who can facilitate introductions.

## 9. Export Logistics — Exporting Green Coffee from Brazil to Japan

### Step-by-Step Process

**Step 1 — Producer Registration and Eligibility**
- Farm must be registered with MAPA (Ministério da Agricultura e Pecuária)
- Producer or exporter must have an active CPFR/CNPJ and be registered in SISCOMEX (Brazil's customs system)
- Membership in a cooperative or exporter association simplifies logistics and documentation

**Step 2 — Coffee Processing and Preparation for Export**
- Green coffee must be hulled, sorted, classified, and bagged in standard 60-kg jute or GrainPro bags
- GrainPro inner liner bags are strongly preferred by Japanese specialty buyers for moisture protection and preservation of aromatics
- Lot traceability: maintain records of farm, variety, harvest date, processing method, and screen size

**Step 3 — Quality Certification**
- SCA cupping score from accredited Q-grader (required for specialty positioning; not legally mandatory but commercially essential)
- ICO Certificate of Origin: required for all coffee exports; issued by Cecafé or authorized entities in Brazil; includes unique identification code (country prefix 002 for Brazil)
- Phytosanitary Certificate: issued by MAPA; certifies the shipment is free of quarantine pests and complies with Japan's plant import requirements
- Organic certificate (if applicable): IBD, Ecocert, or equivalent; must comply with JAS equivalence if labeling "organic" in Japan

**Step 4 — Export Documentation (Full Set)**

| Document | Issuing Authority |
|---|---|
| Commercial Invoice | Exporter |
| Packing List | Exporter |
| Bill of Lading (Sea) or Air Waybill | Shipping carrier |
| ICO Certificate of Origin | Cecafé / authorized issuer |
| Phytosanitary Certificate | MAPA (Ministério da Agricultura) |
| Registro de Exportação (RE) | Filed in SISCOMEX by exporter/broker |
| Certificate of Analysis / Cupping Report | Q-Grader / accredited lab |
| Organic Certificate (if applicable) | Certifying body (IBD, Ecocert, etc.) |
| USDA TM-11 Export Certificate (organic to Japan) | USDA AMS (if using USDA Organic equivalence path) |

**Step 5 — Shipping**
- **Primary export port:** Port of Santos (Santos, SP) — handles 71% of Brazil's coffee export value by FOB
- **Secondary options:** Port of Vitória (ES) for Espírito Santo farms; Port of Paranaguá (PR) for southern producers
- **Shipping method:** Sea freight in 20-foot container (FCL) or grouped consolidation (LCL) for smaller volumes
- **Transit time:** Santos to Yokohama/Tokyo: approximately **20–28 days**
- **Container spec:** Standard dry container; GrainPro inner bags recommended; palletized or floor-loaded per buyer preference
- **Coffee bags per 20' FCL:** ~275 bags of 60 kg = approximately 16,500 kg per container

**Step 6 — Japan Import Clearance**
- Japanese importer handles customs clearance at the port of entry (Tokyo Bay / Yokohama, Osaka)
- Japan Customs requires: Commercial Invoice, Bill of Lading, Phytosanitary Certificate, and any organic certificates
- Food Safety inspection: Japan's Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare (MHLW) may conduct random inspection for pesticide residue compliance under the Positive List system — ensure farms use only approved agrochemicals and maintain spray records
- No import duty on green coffee beans in Japan (0% MFN tariff under HS 0901.11 and 0901.12)

### Logistics Cost Estimate (per 20-foot FCL)

| Cost Item | Estimated Cost (USD) |
|---|---|
| Inland transport (farm to Santos) | $300–1,200 |
| Port handling and export fees (Santos) | $400–800 |
| Ocean freight (Santos → Yokohama, 2026) | $2,500–4,500 |
| Origin documentation (ICO, phyto, RE) | $150–400 |
| Destination handling (CIF to delivered) | $500–1,000 |
| **Total logistics per FCL (~275 bags)** | **$3,850–7,900** |
| **Per 60-kg bag** | **~$14–29** |
| **Per kg** | **~$0.23–0.48** |

Note: Port of Santos has experienced significant congestion-related delays and surcharges since 2024; budget for up to 3–4 weeks of possible delay and include force majeure clauses in export contracts.

## 10. Margin Analysis — Cost vs. Selling Price

### Scenario A: Good Specialty (85 SCA, Natural Yellow Bourbon, Cerrado Mineiro)

| Item | Cost (BRL/60-kg bag) | Cost (USD/60-kg bag) |
|---|---|---|
| Farm gate (producer price) | BRL 1,200–1,600 | $220–295 |
| Processing / cooperative fee | BRL 80–150 | $15–28 |
| Export logistics per bag (Santos FOB) | BRL 100–180 | $18–33 |
| Documentation and certification | BRL 30–60 | $5–11 |
| **Total FOB Brazil** | **BRL 1,410–1,990** | **~$258–367** |
| **FOB price per kg** | | **$4.30–6.12/kg** |

Japanese importer CIF price (add freight ~$0.35/kg + insurance):

| | USD/kg | JPY/kg (¥150/USD) |
|---|---|---|
| CIF Japan | $4.65–6.50 | ¥697–975 |
| Importer margin (15–25%) | $0.70–1.63 | ¥105–244 |
| **Roaster purchase price** | **$5.35–8.13** | **¥802–1,220** |

**Producer FOB margin:** After covering production costs (typically BRL 600–900/bag for well-run farms), the net margin per bag for a 85-SCA lot is approximately **BRL 510–1,090 ($93–200) per bag**. At 275 bags per FCL, this equals **BRL 140,000–300,000 ($25,600–55,000) gross margin per container.**

### Scenario B: Premium Specialty (88 SCA, Washed, Mantiqueira de Minas)

| | USD/60-kg bag | USD/kg |
|---|---|---|
| Farm gate | $400–550 | $6.67–9.17 |
| Processing + logistics to FOB | $60–100 | $1.00–1.67 |
| **FOB Brazil** | **$460–650** | **$7.67–10.83** |
| CIF Japan | $500–700 | $8.33–11.67 |
| Japanese roaster purchase price | $600–900 | $10–15 |
| Roaster retail (100g roasted bag) | | ¥2,500–4,500 |

**Producer FOB margin:** Net margin after production costs (~$350–450/bag) is approximately **$50–200/bag** — tighter per-bag but commands higher absolute price and opens doors to top-tier relationships worth multiples of this in subsequent seasons.

### Scenario C: COE Auction Winner (90+ SCA)

- Auction-determined pricing; 2024 Brazil top lot cleared at ~$232/kg
- No formula applies; production cost becomes nearly irrelevant relative to auction price
- Realistic target: scoring 88–89+ and entering the COE program is achievable for motivated farms; winning or placing in the top 10 produces auction prices of $50–232/kg, versus a production cost of under $8/kg
- **This is the highest-ROI strategy for a single premium lot**, though it requires consistent investment in Q-grader evaluation, processing infrastructure, and possibly a processing specialist

### Key Margin Insight

The Japanese market pays approximately **10–25% above the global specialty average** for equivalent quality. A lot that clears $5/kg in Germany may fetch $6–6.50/kg CIF Japan. The combination of Japan's premium buyer culture, high consumer tolerance for price, and strong yen-denominated retail pricing means that targeting Japan specifically — rather than treating it as just another export destination — produces meaningfully better net revenue per bag over a 3–5 year direct trade relationship.

## 11. Competition — Other Origins and How to Differentiate Brazil

### Ethiopia
- **Position in Japan:** The most beloved single-origin in Japanese specialty cafes; Yirgacheffe and Sidamo are household names among Tokyo coffee enthusiasts
- **Profile:** Bright, floral, bergamot, jasmine, blueberry (naturals), lemon-citrus and tea-like (washed)
- **Brazil's disadvantage vs. Ethiopia:** Cannot compete on floral brightness or acidity
- **Brazil's advantage:** Price stability (Ethiopia supply is volatile), consistent annual harvests, large reliable lots, lower inherent pesticide risk, and the ability to offer large-volume natural/honey lots that roasters can use year-round as a blend anchor

### Colombia
- **Position:** Second most-recognized single-origin in Japan; "Colombian coffee" has global brand recognition
- **Profile:** Balanced, medium body, red apple, caramel, mild citrus; clean and approachable
- **Brazil's disadvantage vs. Colombia:** Colombia's brand recognition and washed profile fit Japan's pour-over preference
- **Brazil's advantage:** Price (Brazil is typically cheaper per kg at equivalent scores), volume reliability, and natural/honey processing options that Colombia rarely offers at scale

### Indonesia (Java, Sumatra, Sulawesi)
- **Position:** Long historical relationship with Japan; earthy, herbal, low-acid profiles popular with older Japanese consumers and for canned coffee production
- **Profile:** Full body, earthy, smoky, low acidity
- **Brazil's advantage over Indonesia:** At specialty tier, Brazil's cleaner processing, higher SCA scores, and better QC infrastructure are clear differentiators; specialty Brazilian natural is better documented and more consistent than Indonesian wet-hulled lots

### Kenya
- **Position:** Cult following among Japanese specialty roasters for SL-28/SL-34 varietal coffees; bright acidity, blackcurrant, tomato, wine notes
- **Volume:** Small — Kenya competes only in the premium tier; not a volume concern
- **Brazil's advantage:** Price and volume; Brazil cannot replicate Kenya's acidity profile but does not need to — different market position

### Panama / Central America (Geisha specifically)
- **Position:** Occupies the ultra-premium tier in Japan; Panama Geisha is the aspirational trophy coffee
- **Brazil's opportunity:** Brazilian Geisha is growing and can offer similar cup profiles at 20–40% lower price than Panama equivalents; this is an underexploited opportunity specifically for Japan

### Differentiation Strategy for Brazil

1. **Volume + Quality Anchor:** Position Brazil as the reliable quality foundation — "the origin Japanese roasters count on year-round as a blend base" — while Ethiopia/Kenya occupy the seasonal excitement role
2. **Natural Processing Heritage:** No other major origin produces natural coffee at Brazil's scale and consistency; own this
3. **Variety Narrative:** Yellow Bourbon and heritage varietals compete directly with Colombia's Caturra/Castillo narrative but with a Brazilian character story
4. **Designation of Origin:** Cerrado Mineiro's DO provides a geographic story that Ethiopian "region" names (Yirgacheffe) already prove works commercially in Japan — apply the same marketing logic
5. **Geisha / Specialty Varietals:** Planting and marketing Brazilian Geisha as a premium alternative to Panama and Colombian Geisha is the highest-ceiling differentiation play
6. **Climate Resilience Story:** Brazil's large-scale investment in climate-adapted varieties and precision agriculture is a sustainability narrative that resonates with younger Japanese consumers

## Practical First Steps — 90-Day Action Plan

1. **Get your SCA score documented.** Hire a certified Q-grader (BSCA can refer) to cup and score your current lots. You need a formal cupping report to approach any Japanese buyer credibly.
2. **Register with BSCA** (Associação Brasileira de Cafés Especiais) and indicate interest in Japan market development programs.
3. **Contact APEX-Brasil** (apexbrasil.com.br) about subsidized SCAJ 2026 participation — application windows typically open 6–9 months before the show (January–April 2026 for SCAJ October 2026).
4. **List on Algrano** (algrano.com) — creates an internationally searchable profile; Japanese roasters actively source through the platform.
5. **Prepare a 1-page lot sheet in English** covering: farm name, region, altitude, variety, processing method, harvest year, SCA score, flavor notes, and available volume in kg. This is the minimum requirement for any buyer conversation.
6. **Send samples proactively.** Budget USD $300–500 for 10–15 DHL sample packages (250g each) to Japanese roasters and importers you identify via LinkedIn and SCAJ exhibitor lists.
7. **Contact Wataru & Co. or Sucafina Japan** as your first commercial importer target — they are specialists who will give you faster feedback than a trading house like Marubeni.
8. **Enter Cup of Excellence** if your lots score 84+. The COE national competition is the single highest-ROI marketing investment available to a Brazilian specialty producer.
