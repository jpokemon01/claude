# US Citizen vs. Japanese National: Owning a Brazilian Trading Company

*Comparison of differences when establishing a Brazilian export/import company.*

---

## Summary

The Brazilian company setup process is essentially the same for both nationalities — but US citizens carry significantly more home-country tax compliance burden.

| Factor | Japanese National | US Citizen | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| CPF & company registration | Standard | Standard | Equal |
| Bank account opening | 4–12 weeks | 8–16 weeks | Japan easier |
| Brazilian tax obligations | Lucro Presumido | Lucro Presumido | Equal |
| Home country tax obligations | Moderate | Heavy (FATCA, Form 5471, FBAR) | Japan much easier |
| Additional home-country CPA cost | None | USD $2,000–5,000/year | Japan cheaper |

---

## Key Difference 1: FATCA — Brazilian Banking Is Harder for US Citizens

**FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act)** is a US law requiring all foreign financial institutions to report accounts held by US citizens to the IRS.

| Issue | Detail |
|---|---|
| Brazilian banks reluctant to open accounts for US citizens | FATCA compliance is expensive — many banks decline US clients |
| Banco do Brasil, Itaú, Bradesco | Comply with FATCA but require extensive documentation and longer KYC review |
| Some smaller banks | Refuse US citizen accounts entirely |
| Account opening time | Expect **8–16 weeks** vs. 4–12 weeks for Japanese nationals |
| Workaround | Use Biz Latin Hub or a Brazilian lawyer with FATCA experience to facilitate |

---

## Key Difference 2: US Tax Reporting Obligations

US citizens are taxed on **worldwide income** regardless of where they live. Owning a Brazilian company triggers additional IRS filings — Japanese nationals do not have these obligations.

| Filing | Trigger | Penalty for Non-Filing |
|---|---|---|
| **Form 5471** | Own 10%+ of a foreign corporation | USD $10,000+ per missed filing |
| **FBAR (FinCEN 114)** | Brazilian bank account balance exceeds USD $10,000 at any point | Up to 50% of account balance |
| **Form 8938 (FATCA)** | Foreign financial assets exceed USD $50,000 | USD $10,000+ penalty |
| **Form 926** | Transfer of cash/property to foreign company | 10% of transferred amount |

**Action required:** Hire a US CPA with international tax experience from day one.
**Additional annual cost:** USD $2,000–5,000/year (on top of Brazilian contador fees).

## What Is the Same for Both Nationalities

| Item | Status |
|---|---|
| CPF application (online, free) | Same |
| Company structure (Ltda / SLU) | Same |
| CNPJ registration | Same |
| RADAR registration (import/export ID) | Same |
| No residency required to own the company | Same |
| Power of Attorney + resident administrator required | Same |
| Tax regime in Brazil (Lucro Presumido) | Same |
| Export tax exemptions (ICMS, PIS, COFINS) | Same |
| Product licenses (MAPA for coffee, ANM for gemstones) | Same |
| Brazilian setup timeline | Same (6–8 months) |

---

## Tax Treaty Status

Neither Japan nor the US has a comprehensive income tax treaty with Brazil — both nationalities are on equal footing on the Brazil side. The difference is entirely on the home-country side (US worldwide income taxation vs. Japan's more limited foreign company reporting requirements).

---

## Recommendation for US Citizens

1. **Hire a US CPA with international tax experience** before incorporating — not after. Get advice on optimal structure (e.g., whether to hold the Brazilian company through a US LLC or directly).
2. **Use a specialist bank facilitator** (Biz Latin Hub or a Brazilian lawyer experienced with FATCA) for bank account opening.
3. **File all IRS forms on time** — penalties for FBAR and Form 5471 are severe and automatic.
4. **Consider renouncing S-corp or partnership structures** in the US before opening a Brazilian company — complex interactions between US pass-through taxation and Brazilian corporate taxation.
